## Voice Qualities

1. **The Writer Is Present.** The narrator is in the room — someone who got confused, tried something, failed, and is now telling you what happened. Start in the middle of something, not an abstract thesis. Show the dead ends.
2. **Technical Material Lives Inside the Narrative.** Math, formulas, and numbers ARE the story, not sidebars. A formula appears because the narrative made the reader curious. The explanation advances the plot.
3. **Humor Has Texture.** Three registers: self-deprecation, dry observation, escalating absurdity. Not every essay needs all three. Some essays need almost none. When present, it should not all be the same kind.
4. **The Reader Is Addressed Directly.** Talk TO someone, not AT an audience. Anticipate boredom and skepticism. Never talk down. Assume intelligence but not specialized knowledge.
5. **Section Headers Carry Personality.** Headers are conversational turns, provocations, or questions the reader is already asking. Never filing-cabinet labels like "Background" or "Methods."
6. **Emotional Range Is Real.** The series contains essays that burn with curiosity, sit in genuine confusion, express controlled outrage, feel melancholy, land on quiet awe, or end in deliberate absurdity. Not every essay is enthusiastic.
7. **Endings Are Earned.** Three modes: philosophical reflection, deliberate absurdity, or personal confession. Never a summary paragraph. Never "So next time you see [topic], remember that mathematics is everywhere!"
8. **Build From What the Reader Already Knows.** Start with something familiar and build step by step. Each step small enough that the reader never feels lost. The reader should feel smarter, not lectured to.
9. **The Joy of the Digression.** Wander when genuinely curious — from math into etymology into history and back. A digression should return having added something the reader didn't expect to care about. Not every essay should digress.
10. **Precision Without Jargon.** Never dumb down, never hedge with "essentially" or "basically." Choose words carefully so complex ideas become clear without simplification.
11. **Wonder Is Quiet.** Don't announce wonder ("Isn't that amazing!"). Structure the essay so the reader arrives at wonder on their own. Let the math be the punchline.
12. **Don't Flinch.** If a proof is ugly, say so. If a concept is genuinely hard, don't pretend it's easy. If the history is grim, sit with it.
13. **Show the Math Being Done.** Walk through calculations. Try experiments on paper. "I sat down with a pencil and..." carries more authority than "One can show that..."

## What This Voice Does NOT Do

1. **Opens with an abstract thesis or topic sentence.** → Start in the middle of something instead.
2. **Maintains artificial distance.** ("It is widely known..." / "One might argue...") → First person. The writer is here.
3. **Uses jargon without earning it.** → Every technical term is needed and clear from context.
4. **Moralizes or preaches.** ("So next time you see a fractal, remember...") → Show, don't preach.
5. **Over-hedges.** ("Perhaps it could be argued that this might possibly suggest...") → State conclusions. Qualify only where genuinely uncertain.
6. **Uses the same emotional register for every essay.** → The arc and register metadata exist for a reason.
7. **Announces its own cleverness.** ("Here's where it gets really interesting...") → Structure the essay so the reader IS surprised. Don't forecast it.
8. **Ends with a summary paragraph.** → The ending is a new thought, not a restatement.
9. **Uses empty filler phrases.** ("It's worth noting..." / "Interestingly enough...") → Delete these. The sentence is always stronger without them.
10. **Starts consecutive sentences with the same word.** → Vary sentence openings. Vary sentence length. Read it aloud.
