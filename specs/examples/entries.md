# Entry Style Examples

Each section shows a sample opening sentence or two. Adapt the technique — do not copy the content.

## cold-open

The helicopter hit the water at 4:17 PM. All six passengers survived. The reason has nothing to do with the pilot and everything to do with a formula written by a Swiss mathematician in 1752.

## on-the-math

Consider two points on a sphere. The shortest path between them is not a straight line — it's an arc of a great circle. This simple fact reshapes everything from airline routes to artillery trajectories.

## question

What would happen if you could fold a piece of paper 42 times? Most people guess you'd get a thick stack. The actual answer — reaching from here to the moon — reveals how badly our brains handle exponential growth.

## contradiction

Light is a wave. Light is also a particle. Both of these statements are experimentally verified. Neither is wrong. If this bothers you, you're in excellent company — it bothered Einstein too.

## setting

The Archive of Unreceived Letters sits in a basement in Prague, two floors below a bakery that sells kolaches. It contains over four thousand pieces of mail that were never delivered. The mathematics of why is a story about networks.

## history

In 1904, a Swedish mathematician named Helge von Koch drew a snowflake that would change geometry forever. The curve he described has infinite length but encloses a finite area — a contradiction that took sixty years to fully resolve.

## at-the-end

By the time you finish this essay, you will know why hospitals schedule surgeries the way they do, why your GPS occasionally sends you on a bizarre detour, and why both of these problems are, mathematically, the same problem. But we need to start somewhere simpler.

## voice

I have a confession to make: I was thirty-four years old before I understood why manhole covers are round. Not "sort of understood." I mean I had genuinely never thought about it, and when someone finally explained it, the answer was so elegant I felt embarrassed.

## data

In 2019, Americans spent an average of 54 minutes per day stuck in traffic. That's 219 hours per year — just over nine full days — sitting in a car going nowhere. The mathematics of traffic flow says roughly half of that time is unnecessary.
