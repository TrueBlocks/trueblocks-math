# Structure Examples

Each section describes the structural pattern and shows a brief sketch. Adapt the pattern — do not copy.

## narrative

Linear storytelling: beginning, middle, end. A single thread from setup through complication to resolution. The math emerges naturally from the story's needs.

## in-medias-res

Open in the middle of the action. The reader is disoriented — intentionally. Fill in backstory only as needed. The opening scene returns at the end, fully understood.

## frame

A wrapper story contains the real story. The narrator is telling someone else about the math, or encountering it inside another context. The frame gives permission to explain.

## braided

Two or three threads — different people, eras, or problems — running in parallel. They intersect at key moments. The reader sees connections the characters don't. Converge at the end.

## reverse-chronology

Start with the result. Work backward to the cause. Each section answers "but why?" for the section before it. The deepest layer is the most surprising.

## compare-contrast

Two things that seem similar (or seem different) are set side by side. The comparison reveals something neither would show alone. Works well for competing theories.

## catalogue

A curated collection of instances, each a mini-story. Order matters — build from familiar to astonishing. The thread connecting them is the real subject.

## exposition

Direct explanation, clearly structured. No pretense of narrative — the essay teaches. Earn the reader's attention with clarity and pacing, not plot.

## argument

A claim is made, evidence is marshaled, objections are addressed. The essay moves toward a verdict. The reader should feel the conclusion is earned, not asserted.

## letter

Written to a specific person. Second-person address ("you") throughout. The tone is patient, generous, slightly intimate. Math arrives as shared discovery.

## field-guide

Organized by specimen: "here is one kind, here is another." Each entry is self-contained but the collection reveals a taxonomy. Works well for classification topics.

## meditation

Slow, reflective, circling a single idea from multiple angles. Not rushing toward a conclusion. The reader sits with the concept and lets understanding accumulate.

## dialogue

Two voices exchange ideas. One may know more than the other. The format allows natural questions and clarifications. Keep it sharp — no filler.

## q-and-a

Explicit question-and-answer format. Each question is one the reader would actually ask. Answers can vary in length. The sequence builds understanding.

## vignette-collage

Short, self-contained scenes — each 200-400 words — arranged to create a composite picture. No explicit transitions between them. Juxtaposition does the work.

## chronicle

Events in chronological order, often spanning years or decades. The timeline itself is part of the argument. Key moments get more space; quiet periods compress.
