# Register Examples

Each section shows a sample paragraph in the given register. Adapt the tone — do not copy the content.

## casual

You know how sometimes you're standing in the grocery store trying to figure out which line will move fastest? You probably just pick the shortest one. Turns out that's almost always the wrong strategy, and the reason why involves a genuinely surprising bit of probability theory.

## academic

The relationship between prime distribution and the zeros of the Riemann zeta function constitutes one of number theory's most profound connections. What makes this relationship remarkable is not merely its mathematical elegance but its implications for the structure of the integers — implications that remain, after more than a century, only partially understood.

## whimsical

If numbers had personalities, seven would be the oddball at the party — literally odd, prime, and stubbornly indivisible. It refuses to play nice with others. Mathematicians love it anyway, which probably says something about mathematicians.

## lyrical

There is a curve that the nautilus builds without knowing it, that the hurricane follows without choosing it, that the galaxy traces across a hundred thousand light-years of empty dark. The logarithmic spiral asks nothing of its host. It simply appears, the way a melody appears in birdsong — unbidden, exact, and everywhere.

## dry

The bridge collapsed on November 7, 1940. The wind was 42 miles per hour — well within design tolerances. The engineers had accounted for static wind load. They had not accounted for aeroelastic flutter. This distinction, which can be stated in about four equations, cost the state of Washington $6.4 million.

## urgent

Right now, somewhere in the Pacific, a wave is building that will arrive at the California coast in exactly 11 hours. We know this because a set of differential equations, first written down in the 1840s, tell us so. Those same equations are also telling us something about next Tuesday that no one wants to hear.

## warm

My daughter asked me at breakfast why the toast always lands butter-side down. I told her it doesn't — it just feels that way. She looked at me with deep suspicion. So we spent the rest of Saturday morning dropping toast off the kitchen table, and what we found was more interesting than either of us expected.

## clinical

The subject pool comprised 1,247 adults, ages 18 to 74. Each was presented with a simple probability puzzle. 89% answered incorrectly. The error was not random — it was systematic, predictable, and it tracked a specific cognitive bias first documented by Kahneman and Tversky in 1979.

## sardonic

Economists will tell you that humans are rational actors. Humans will tell you they bought a lottery ticket because it "felt lucky." The mathematics of expected value has an opinion about this, and it is not diplomatic.

## wonder-struck

There are more possible arrangements of a deck of cards than there are atoms in the Earth. Not metaphorically — literally. Every time you shuffle a deck properly, you are almost certainly holding an arrangement that has never existed before and will never exist again. Hold it for a moment. You're holding a one-of-a-kind object.
