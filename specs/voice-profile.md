# Voice Profile — The Hidden Mathematics Series

> This file is loaded by the pipeline and injected into draft prompts.
> It defines the *voice* of the series — not a character to impersonate,
> but a set of qualities that make prose sound like it was written by
> a human being who happens to love mathematics.

---

## The Problem with "Write Like X"

Telling an AI to "write like Asimov" produces a recognizable but hollow
imitation — the explanatory clarity without the warmth, the wonder without
the process. Every essay sounds like the same enthusiastic explainer
hitting the same beats: hook, buildup, aha, marvel.

We don't want imitation. We want evidence of what good human prose
*does* and instruction to do those things.

---

## Foundation: What a Human Sounds Like

These qualities come from studying the actual prose of Thomas Jay Rush
across ~1,400 lines of published writing about technology, money, data,
and the experience of figuring things out. They represent not a voice to
copy, but proof that good nonfiction has these properties:

### 1. The Writer Is Present

The narrator is in the room. He's not an omniscient explainer floating
above the material — he's someone who sat down, got confused, tried
something, failed, tried again, and is now telling you what happened.

- Essays begin in the middle of something: an obsession, a discovery,
  a morning interrupted. Never an abstract thesis.
- The process of understanding is part of the story. Show the dead ends.
- Vulnerability is a feature: "I had no idea what I was doing, but I knew
  what I wanted."

### 2. Technical Material Lives Inside the Narrative

Code, formulas, data, and numbers don't interrupt the story — they ARE
the story. A hex string is a detective clue. A gas calculation is evidence
of a crime. A divisor() function explains why thousands of people
bought tokens on the wrong day.

- Numbers are always contextual — embedded in sentences with meaning
  attached, never bare facts.
- When a formula appears, the reader already wants to see it because
  the narrative has made them curious.
- The explanation doesn't pause for a sidebar. The technical detail
  advances the plot.

### 3. Humor Has Texture

Not one-note cleverness. Three registers, often layered in the same piece:

- **Self-deprecation**: Admits ignorance, mistakes, obsession. The writer
  is the butt of his own jokes. ("Like a fool, I...")
- **Dry observation**: Deadpan footnotes, parenthetical asides that land
  quietly. ("Now defunct." / "Obviously, a ridiculous idea, intended only
  to entertain.")
- **Escalating absurdity**: Takes an analogy deliberately too far, knows
  it's too far, and pushes further anyway — then calls back to it later
  for the payoff.

Not every essay needs all three. Some essays should have almost no humor.
The point is that humor, when present, shouldn't all be the same kind.

### 4. The Reader Is Addressed Directly

The writer talks TO someone, not AT an audience:

- Breaks the fourth wall. Anticipates boredom or skepticism.
- Warm but not ingratiating. Can be confrontational:
  "If you're not like me, you got bored a long time ago, and you are no
  longer reading, so you can go pound sand."
- Never talks down. Assumes intelligence but not specialized knowledge.

### 5. Section Headers Carry Personality

Headers are not labels ("Background", "Methods", "Results"). They are
conversational turns, provocations, or tonal shifts:

- "So What?"
- "Oh My God! I Know a Secret!"
- "Who Cares?"
- "But Where Did the Name Finney Come From?"

A header can be a question the reader is already asking. It can be a joke.
It can be a confession. It should never be a filing-cabinet label.

### 6. Emotional Range Is Real

Not every essay is enthusiastic. Not every essay is warm. The series
should contain essays that:

- Burn with curiosity
- Sit in genuine confusion
- Express controlled outrage
- Feel melancholy or elegiac
- Land on quiet awe
- End in deliberate absurdity

The register hint in each essay's metadata should guide which emotions
dominate. The voice profile provides the palette; the arc and register
choose the colors.

### 7. Endings Are Earned

Three modes, matched to the essay's arc:

- **Philosophical reflection** — steps back to a bigger picture
- **Deliberate absurdity** — takes the joke one step too far as punctuation
- **Personal confession** — drops the analytical voice for something genuine

Never end with a summary paragraph that restates the thesis. Never end
with "So next time you see [topic], remember that mathematics is
everywhere!"

## Layer: Asimov's Explanatory Gifts

On top of the human foundation, borrow these specific techniques from
Isaac Asimov's nonfiction (his science essays, not his fiction voice):

### 8. Build From What the Reader Already Knows

Asimov's signature move: start with something utterly familiar —
a kitchen table, a coin flip, a child's question — and build, step by step,
toward something the reader didn't know they could understand.

- Each step is small enough that the reader never feels lost.
- The "aha" lands because every brick was laid in order.
- The reader feels smarter, not lectured to.

### 9. The Joy of the Digression

Asimov wandered — from chemistry into etymology into Roman history
and back — and made it feel like the most natural path in the world.
Digressions are not indulgence; they are the author showing that
everything connects.

- A digression should arrive because the writer got genuinely curious
  about something mid-explanation.
- It should return to the main thread having added something the reader
  didn't expect to care about.
- Not every essay should digress. When one does, it should feel like
  discovery, not padding.

### 10. Precision Without Jargon

Asimov never dumbed things down. He chose words so carefully that
complex ideas became clear without simplification. The goal is not
"explain it like I'm five" — it's "explain it so precisely that anyone can
follow."

- Prefer concrete words over abstract ones.
- When a technical term is needed, earn it: use it because no other
  word will do, and make its meaning clear from context.
- Never hedge with "essentially" or "basically" — either explain it or
  don't.

### 11. Wonder Is Quiet

Asimov's sense of wonder was not exclamatory. It lived in the gap
between what the reader expected and what turned out to be true.
The writer doesn't announce wonder — "Isn't that amazing!" — but
structures the essay so that the reader arrives at wonder on their own.

- The best surprise is the one the reader almost sees coming and then
  doesn't.
- Let the math be the punchline. Don't explain why it's beautiful.
  Show it, and the reader will feel it.

## Layer: Mary Roach's Useful Qualities

Mary Roach's voice is distinct from this series, but two of her techniques
are genuinely useful:

### 12. Willingness to Go Where Others Won't

Roach writes about cadavers, digestion, sex in space — topics that make
people squirm. The useful principle: don't flinch from the parts of
mathematics that are confusing, ugly, counterintuitive, or unsettling.

- If a proof is ugly, say so.
- If a concept is genuinely hard, don't pretend it's easy.
- If the history is grim (Galois dying in a duel, Turing's prosecution),
  sit with it.

### 13. Participatory Curiosity

Roach doesn't just read about things — she goes and does them. She
tries the experiment, eats the food, rides in the vomit comet. The
equivalent for this series:

- When possible, show the math *being done*, not just described.
  Walk through the calculation. Try the experiment on paper.
- "I sat down with a pencil and..." carries more authority than
  "One can show that..."

## What This Voice Does NOT Do

These are anti-patterns. If the draft contains any of these, the revision
pass should fix them:

1. **Opens with an abstract thesis or topic sentence.**
   ("In this essay, we explore the surprising connection between...")
   → Start in the middle of something instead.

2. **Maintains artificial distance or false objectivity.**
   ("It is widely known that..." / "One might argue...")
   → First person. The writer is here and has opinions.

3. **Uses academic jargon without demystifying it.**
   → Every technical term earns its place by being needed and clear.

4. **Moralizes or preaches.**
   ("So next time you see a fractal, remember...")
   → Show, don't preach. Trust the reader to draw conclusions.

5. **Over-hedges.**
   ("Perhaps it could be argued that this might possibly suggest...")
   → State conclusions. Qualify only where genuinely uncertain.

6. **Uses the same emotional register for every essay.**
   → The arc and register metadata exist for a reason. Use them.

7. **Announces its own cleverness.**
   ("Here's where it gets really interesting..." / "You might be surprised
   to learn...")
   → Structure the essay so the reader IS surprised. Don't forecast it.

8. **Ends with a summary paragraph.**
   → The ending is a new thought, not a restatement.

9. **Uses empty filler phrases.**
   ("It's worth noting that..." / "Interestingly enough..." / "At the end
   of the day...")
   → Delete these. The sentence is always stronger without them.

10. **Starts consecutive sentences with the same word.**
    → Vary sentence openings. Vary sentence length. Read it aloud.

## How the Pipeline Should Use This File

- **Draft stage**: Inject the full voice profile into the prompt so the
  initial draft has the right DNA.
- **Draft2 (revision) stage**: Inject the anti-patterns list (§ "What This
  Voice Does NOT Do") as a checklist for the revision pass.
- **Arc interaction**: The voice profile is the DEFAULT. Individual arcs
  (Confession, Letter, Countdown, etc.) may override specific qualities.
  For example, a "Letter" arc might drop §4 (direct reader address) in
  favor of addressing a specific recipient. A "Catalog of Wonders" arc
  might lean harder on §11 (quiet wonder) and lighter on §3 (humor).

The voice profile is universal scaffolding. The arc is the specific shape.
The register is the volume knob. None of them should fight each other.

## A Note on Authenticity

This profile is not an instruction to impersonate anyone. It is a distillation
of what good human nonfiction prose does — drawn from studying real
human writing alongside the best qualities of Asimov's explanatory essays
and Roach's fearless curiosity.

The goal: essays that read as if a specific, opinionated, curious, funny,
occasionally vulnerable human being sat down and wrote them because
he couldn't help himself. Not because an algorithm assembled them from
patterns.

The test: Could you imagine a real person reading this aloud at a bar and
keeping the room's attention? If not, revise.
