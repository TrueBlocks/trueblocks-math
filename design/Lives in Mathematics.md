# Lives in Mathematics

> A small collection of stories where the *person* illuminates the *idea* — biographical
> essays with a mathematical spine. Each centers on a human moment that changed how we
> understand something. Not hagiography. Mistakes, failures, and stubbornness are the point.

---

## 1. Euler's Blindness and Productivity

**The person:** Leonhard Euler (1707–1783).  
**The moment:** He lost sight in his right eye at 28, went fully blind at 59 — and his output *increased*. Half of his 866 papers were written blind, dictated to assistants.  
**The math:** Euler touched everything — number theory, graph theory, calculus of variations, fluid dynamics, optics. He named most of the notation we use ($e$, $i$, $f(x)$, $\pi$, $\Sigma$). His work on the bridges of Königsberg invented topology.  
**Why it's an essay:** The most prolific mathematician in history did his best work without sight. What does it mean that mathematics can be done entirely in the mind?

## 2. Emmy Noether and the Theorem Nobody Noticed

**The person:** Emmy Noether (1882–1935).  
**The moment:** She proved the deepest theorem in physics while forbidden from holding a faculty position. Hilbert, frustrated, said: "I do not see that the sex of the candidate is an argument against her admission. After all, we are a university, not a bathhouse."  
**The math:** Noether's theorem (1918): every continuous symmetry corresponds to a conservation law. It unifies all of classical and quantum physics. She also founded modern abstract algebra.  
**Why it's an essay:** The most important theorem in physics was proved by someone the physics establishment refused to hire.

## 3. Ramanujan's Notebooks

**The person:** Srinivasa Ramanujan (1887–1920).  
**The moment:** A letter from an unknown clerk in Madras to G.H. Hardy at Cambridge, January 1913. Nine pages of results — many new, some known, several "almost incredible." Hardy: "They must be true because no one would have the imagination to invent them."  
**The math:** Partition functions, continued fractions, mock theta functions. Many of his conjectures were proved decades later using tools he never had. The taxicab number 1729 = $12^3 + 1^3 = 10^3 + 9^3$.  
**Why it's an essay:** Mathematics as intuition so deep it bypasses proof. Ramanujan said the goddess Namagiri wrote equations on his tongue. What does "discovery" mean when it arrives without derivation?

## 4. Galois's Last Night

**The person:** Évariste Galois (1811–1832).  
**The moment:** The night before a duel (over politics, or a woman — the record is unclear), Galois wrote a 60-page letter to his friend Auguste Chevalier, containing the foundations of group theory. In the margin: "I have no time. I have no time." He died the next morning at 20.  
**The math:** Galois theory connects field extensions to group symmetry, proving that quintic equations have no general formula. It took 14 years for anyone to understand his letter.  
**Why it's an essay:** A teenager invented a branch of mathematics in a single night because he believed he was about to die. He was right.

## 5. Ada Lovelace and the First Program

**The person:** Ada Lovelace (1815–1852).  
**The moment:** Translating Menabrea's article on Babbage's Analytical Engine, she added notes three times longer than the original — including what is now considered the first computer program (computing Bernoulli numbers).  
**The math:** Note G contains a step-by-step algorithm with loops and conditional branching, a century before any physical computer existed. She also wrote: "The Engine has no pretensions to *originate* anything. It can only do *whatever we know how to order it to perform.*"  
**Why it's an essay:** The first person to see that a calculating machine could do more than calculate. Her caveat about origination is still the central question of AI.

## 6. Cantor's Diagonals and Madness

**The person:** Georg Cantor (1845–1918).  
**The moment:** In 1891, he proved that there are more real numbers than natural numbers — infinity comes in sizes. Kronecker attacked him publicly ("a corrupter of youth"). Cantor spent the last decades of his life in and out of psychiatric institutions.  
**The math:** The diagonal argument: assume a complete list of real numbers between 0 and 1. Construct a number not on the list by changing the $n$th digit of the $n$th entry. Contradiction. Therefore: $|\mathbb{R}| > |\mathbb{N}|$. The continuum hypothesis — is there a set between these two infinities? — is *independent* of standard axioms (Gödel 1940, Cohen 1963).  
**Why it's an essay:** A proof so simple a child can follow it, conclusions so strange they drove its creator to despair, and a question it raised that turned out to be *unanswerable*.

## 7. Florence Nightingale's Rose Diagram

**The person:** Florence Nightingale (1820–1910).  
**The moment:** Returning from the Crimean War, she needed Parliament to fund sanitary reforms. She invented the polar area diagram ("coxcomb") to show that soldiers died mostly from preventable disease, not battle wounds. The visualization worked — Parliament acted.  
**The math:** A pioneer of statistical graphics before the term existed. She was elected the first female Fellow of the Royal Statistical Society in 1858. Her innovation: making data *legible to power*.  
**Why it's an essay:** Mathematics as political persuasion. A nurse proved that pie charts save lives.

## 8. Turing's Halting Problem and His Life

**The person:** Alan Turing (1912–1954).  
**The moment:** In 1936, Turing proved that no general algorithm can decide whether an arbitrary program will halt or run forever. This *negative* result founded computer science. He went on to crack Enigma, conceive the Turing machine, and propose the Turing test. In 1952, he was prosecuted for homosexuality and subjected to chemical castration. He died of cyanide poisoning in 1954.  
**The math:** The halting problem is the computational analog of Gödel's incompleteness — there are questions about programs that programs cannot answer. Yet Turing's practical work (breaking Enigma, designing the ACE computer) was staggeringly effective. He moved freely between the impossible and the pragmatic.  
**Why it's an essay:** The man who proved that some problems are unsolvable then went on to solve one of history's hardest practical problems. His country thanked him with destruction.

## 9. Maryam Mirzakhani's Surfaces

**The person:** Maryam Mirzakhani (1977–2017).  
**The moment:** In 2014, she became the first woman to win the Fields Medal, for her work on the dynamics and geometry of Riemann surfaces and their moduli spaces. She described her mathematical process as "like being lost in a jungle and trying to use all the knowledge that you can gather to come up with some new tricks."  
**The math:** She connected hyperbolic geometry, complex analysis, and dynamical systems. Her work on counting simple closed geodesics on surfaces was described as "a masterpiece of mathematical architecture."  
**Why it's an essay:** Her working style — filling huge sheets of paper with pictures while her daughter called it "painting" — captures what mathematical creativity actually looks like. Not symbols on a blackboard. Exploration.

## 10. Perelman's Refusal

**The person:** Grigori Perelman (b. 1966).  
**The moment:** In 2002–2003, he posted three papers on arXiv proving the Poincaré conjecture (and the more general Geometrization conjecture) — a Millennium Prize problem. He then declined the Fields Medal and the $1 million prize, withdrew from mathematics entirely, and lives in Saint Petersburg with his mother.  
**The math:** The Poincaré conjecture: every simply connected, closed 3-manifold is homeomorphic to the 3-sphere. Perelman used Ricci flow with surgery (Hamilton's program). His proof took years for the mathematical community to verify.  
**Why it's an essay:** Someone solved one of the seven hardest problems in mathematics, then walked away from all recognition. What does mathematics mean to someone for whom it is truly sufficient in itself?
