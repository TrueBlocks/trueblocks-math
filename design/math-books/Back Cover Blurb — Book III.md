# Back Cover Blurb — Book III: *The Edge of Knowing*

Ice is slippery. You learned that before you could read. Physicists still cannot explain why.

Hot water sometimes freezes faster than cold. The coastline of Britain is either 17,820 kilometers or infinitely long, depending on your ruler. A cat with zero angular momentum somehow rotates 180 degrees in three-tenths of a second. And pouring honey from a jar involves equations so difficult that solving them is worth a million dollars.

*The Edge of Knowing* is about the frontier — the place where familiar things become mysterious and simple questions turn out to have no answers. These 34 essays travel from the things we thought we understood, through the patterns that emerge at every scale, past the geometry that breaks intuition, and into the quantum threshold where measurement itself becomes the problem. They end with four portraits of mathematics as a human endeavor: a shipping clerk in Madras writing the most important letter in mathematical history, a twenty-year-old scrawling group theory by candlelight the night before his duel, and the still-unanswered question of why abstract mathematics describes physical reality at all.

The first book showed you the math around you. The second revealed the architecture beneath it. This one takes you to the edge — where the boundary between what we know and what we don't keeps moving, and the not-knowing is the most exciting part.
