# Cover Image Designs — New Math Series

Three covers, one visual language. Each cover uses the same composition and palette family so the books read as a series on a shelf, but each image shifts in subject, scale, and mood to match its book's thesis.

---

## Shared Design Principles

**Composition:** Each cover is a single, centered object or scene — no collage, no busy borders, no overlapping elements. The object sits in generous white or warm off-white space. Think of it as a photograph of one thing, taken from close range, with soft natural light from the upper left.

**Palette family:** Rich but light. Warm mid-tones — terra cotta, sage, goldenrod, slate blue, warm gray — never neon, never black-dominant. Shadows are soft and warm (warm gray or muted lavender), not harsh. Highlights lean slightly golden, as if lit by late-afternoon sun through a window. The overall impression should be closer to a watercolor study than a textbook diagram — but not washed-out or pale. The colors should feel *comfortable* and *Adult*.

**Typography zone:** The bottom third of each cover is left relatively clear for the title, subtitle, and author name. The image occupies the upper two-thirds and bleeds gently into the title zone without a hard line — the object fades or the background lightens as it approaches the bottom edge.

**Series mark:** A small, consistent geometric element in the same position on all three covers — a thin circle, perhaps 12mm diameter, positioned in the upper-right corner. Inside the circle: the Roman numeral I, II, or III in a clean serif face. Same warm gray on all three. This is the only element that explicitly marks the volume number.

**No text in the image itself.** The image is purely visual. Title and author are handled by the typographer separately.

## Book I — *Everything Is a Rate of Change*

### The Image: A Single Soap Bubble on a Kitchen Table

**What you see:** A single soap bubble, roughly the size of a grapefruit, resting on a smooth wooden kitchen table. Morning light enters from the upper left, passing through the bubble and casting a soft, warped rainbow on the wood grain beneath it. The bubble is not perfectly centered — it sits slightly left of center, as if it just landed. A faint reflection of a window is visible in the bubble's upper curve. The table extends to the edges of the frame, slightly out of focus at the corners.

**Why this image:** Book I is about the mathematics hidden in what you can touch, hear, and see — your kitchen, your body, your neighborhood. The soap bubble is literally from the essay "The Geometry of Soap Bubbles" (I.03.04), but it also embodies the entire book's premise: something ordinary and ephemeral that contains deep mathematics (minimal surfaces, Plateau's rules, the double-bubble conjecture). It is domestic, warm, and surprising.

**Color notes:**
- The bubble itself is largely transparent, with thin arcs of spectral color — not a garish full rainbow, but soft bands of rose-gold, pale sage green, and a thin line of slate blue where the film is thinnest
- The table is a warm, honey-toned oak with visible but gentle grain
- The background is soft, out-of-focus warm white — a kitchen wall, perhaps, with the faintest suggestion of a window frame
- The cast rainbow on the table is real but subtle: a crescent of diffracted color, mostly pinks and greens, soft-edged
- Overall tone: warm, golden, quiet morning

**Mood:** Intimate. Still. The kind of moment you might actually see at breakfast if you looked closely enough.

**What to avoid:** Do not add other objects to the table (no coffee mug, no book, no flowers). Do not show hands or people. Do not stylize the bubble into a diagram or schematic. It should look like a photograph, not an illustration.

## Book II — *The Hidden Architecture*

### The Image: A Nautilus Shell, Cross-Sectioned, on a Linen Cloth

**What you see:** A nautilus shell that has been cut cleanly in half, revealing the internal logarithmic spiral of chambers. The shell sits on a piece of natural, slightly rumpled linen, oriented so the spiral opens to the right. The chambers decrease in size toward the center with the mathematical precision that is the actual subject of essay II.06.01. Light enters from the upper left, illuminating the outer chambers in warm cream and peach tones, while the innermost chambers recede into soft shadow. The mother-of-pearl interior catches light along the chamber walls in thin lines of rose and silver.

**Why this image:** Book II is about the invisible structures that organize the world — the architecture beneath the surface. A cross-sectioned nautilus is the literal inside view: the structure you don't see when you hold the shell whole. The logarithmic spiral is one of mathematics' most recognizable curves, and the chambered progression embodies the book's themes of hidden pattern, self-similarity, and elegant scaling. It belongs directly to "The Logarithmic Spiral of Seashells" (II.06.01) but speaks to the entire volume: Benford's law, the Pareto distribution, Shannon entropy — all are hidden internal structures made visible.

**Color notes:**
- Outer shell surface: warm cream with faint bands of terra cotta and amber, like old parchment
- Inner chamber walls: pale peach to rose, with thin highlights of silver-white where the nacre catches light
- Deep center: soft warm shadow, not black — more like a muted burgundy fading to warm dark gray
- Linen beneath: natural off-white with a very slight warm gray texture, gentle folds catching soft shadow
- Background: same warm off-white as Book I, maintaining series continuity
- Overall tone: warm, elegant, the feeling of holding something old and precise

**Mood:** Contemplative. Reverent. Like finding a perfect specimen on a beach walk and holding it up to the light.

**What to avoid:** Do not place the shell on sand or a beach. Do not add water, starfish, or other shells. Do not add a ruler or any measuring instrument. Do not diagram golden-ratio overlays onto the spiral. The mathematics should be implicit in the form, not annotated.

## Book III — *The Edge of Knowing*

### The Image: A Single Snowflake on Dark Blue Wool

**What you see:** A single snowflake — large enough to see its six-fold branching structure with the unaided eye — resting on a piece of deep blue-gray wool fabric (like a mitten or scarf). The snowflake is a stellar dendrite: six primary arms, each with secondary branches that echo the primary structure at a smaller scale. It is not a perfect textbook diagram but a real crystal — slightly asymmetric, with one arm a touch shorter than the others, a few branches not quite matching their counterparts. Light comes from directly above, making the ice crystal glow against the darker fabric. Tiny glints of refracted light appear at a few branch tips, like miniature stars.

**Why this image:** Book III is about the frontier — the things we almost understand, the edge where pattern meets mystery. The snowflake is from "Symmetry in Snowflakes" (I.04.05, originally in Book I) but its meaning shifts against the dark wool. It sits at the intersection of perfect mathematical symmetry (hexagonal ice lattice, group theory, dendritic growth governed by diffusion) and irreducible uniqueness (no two are alike, the growth history is unrepeatable). This tension — between the law and the particular, between what we can predict and what we can't — is exactly Book III's thesis. The dark fabric is the unknown. The crystal is what we've understood so far. It is already melting.

**Color notes:**
- The wool fabric: deep blue-gray, the color of a winter twilight — rich but not black (approximately the darkest element in the series, but still clearly blue, not dark)
- The snowflake: translucent crystalline white with the faintest blue-white tint, catching light along its edges
- Refracted glints at branch tips: tiny points of warm white and the palest gold
- Fabric texture: visible wool fibers, slightly fuzzy, providing warm organic contrast to the crystal's geometry
- Background above the fabric: gradual fade to the series' warm off-white, so the dark wool doesn't dominate the entire cover
- Overall tone: cooler than Books I and II, but still warm enough to belong to the same family — twilight rather than midnight

**Mood:** Wonder. A held breath. The feeling of catching something beautiful and fragile before it disappears.

**What to avoid:** Do not make the snowflake a perfect geometric diagram — it should look real, photographed, with the slight imperfections of actual crystal growth. Do not add other snowflakes. Do not add falling snow in the background. Do not show a hand or finger holding the fabric. The single crystal, alone on the dark wool, is the entire image.

## How the Three Covers Work Together

When the three books stand side by side on a shelf, the covers progress:

1. **Warm and close** — a soap bubble on a honey-toned kitchen table (domestic, morning, golden light)
2. **Warm and reverent** — a nautilus spiral on natural linen (structural, afternoon, rose-and-cream light)
3. **Cool and still** — a snowflake on dark blue wool (frontier, twilight, crystalline light)

The palette shifts gradually from warm gold (Book I) through warm rose (Book II) to cool blue-white (Book III), mirroring the series arc: from the familiar kitchen to the invisible architecture to the edge of knowing. Each image is a single natural object. Each contains visible mathematics without any annotation. Each is simple enough to read from across a bookstore.

The shared elements — generous white space, upper-left light source, soft shadows, the small Roman-numeral circle in the upper right, the same warm off-white background family — hold them together as a series. The differences — subject, temperature, and mood — tell the reader that each book goes somewhere new.
